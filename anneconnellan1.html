We find Anne Connellan, Michael’s widow referenced in various official documents and newspaper articles from 1834 onwards.

This decade also saw family deaths for Anne. She lost her husband Michael between 1829 and 1833. Her father (or her grand-father) Peter O’Neill died in 1832, her brother (or uncle) Felix died in 1835 and her mother (or grand-mother) Alicia died in 1838.  Was Felix’s wife alive at the time of his death? If not did his son, Peter go to live with Anne. There is no O’Neill living in Meigh at the time of  Griffith’s Valuation in 1864.


These were also the years of the marriages of the two sons we can definitely attribute to her, Laurence in 1832 and Patrick in 1848. The 16 year gap between the two marriages would seem to indicate a similarly large gap in their ages, which in turn would indicate other children in between who didn’t marry or who died. Any knowledge we have can only be based on the documentation currently available.


In 1834, Anne Connellan is listed in the Tithe Applotment Books for Meigh as the Widow Connolly.  The entries show her as renting two other properties as well as the main dwelling.  These two further lots, will be reflected in the census listings in later years.


In the Townland Valuations of 1836 and 1838 we again have reference to Anne Connellan. This Townland Valuation was carried out between 1828 and 1840. It was a land valuation but it also included houses with an annual valuation of £3 or more.  So in the valuation of lands held by Jonathan Seaver of Heath Hall, we have the townland of Meigh with descriptions of stony fields, heath and boggy pasture.  There is mention of only two houses, Anne Connellan valued at £4.18.00 and Patrick Mallon valued at £3.8.00. The third building mentioned is the Church valued at £15. This would be Church of Ireland on the Dromintee Road, not Meigh Chapel which wasn’t built until 1852.  


In the Land Valuation for Clonlum, we again find only two houses mentioned, that of John O’Neill and the landlord Powal Foxall. By 1936, John O’Neill was the acknowledged householder. His father had died in 1832, his brothers had left home or it could be because he was the eldest son.


One is tempted to draw a comparison with the marriage of Catherine Lennon and Bernard Murphy of Clonalig and observe that the children of higher value rentals tend to marry one another. Hence a marriage between an O’Neill from Clonlum and a Connellan in Meigh. The marriage of Anne’s eldest grand-child, also Anne, to Henry Colgan of Aghayalloge is similar.


This land valuation was carried out by Charles Pollock and on the final page for Meigh his comment is significant, telling us that the rents paid to the landlord were well above the value of the land.



Some weeks  later another paragraph in the same paper gives us further information about this episode. We learn that some of the soldiers involved in surveying the area around Meigh had been billeted at the Connellan public house whether that was sleeping in the house or more likely camping in the grounds, we don’t know. It seems there had been a night of carousing in the pub involving some locals and two of the soldiers working on the survey which was broken up at 2.00 in the morning by the police from Forkhill.  The above mentioned John O’Donovan as one of the supervisors of the scheme could well have been around at the time of this incident. His letter mentioning John O’Neill was written the following year.

These were troubled times. Life was hard for the tenant farmers of the Seaver estate who were paying 250 per cent above its value. Inequality was rife at this time and Catholics had few rights and resented paying tithes to the protestant church.  Many supported the secret but outlawed society of Ribbonism, founded in the previous century with the aim of fighting injustices and for tenants rights. Its members carried out attacks on tithe and process servers, on property and  on any tenant who took over the property of an unjustly evicted tenant. Ribbonism was so called because its members wore a green ribbon in a button-hole.


In 1839 there is a paragraph repeated in a number of papers of a search for ribbon papers in the house of the widow Connellan in Meigh




In 1836 we have a very graphic newspaper account of how after a Saturday fair in Jonesborough there were scenes of rioting and drunkeness all along the roads to Meigh and that this continued until six on the Sunday morning, the public house in Meigh seemingly being open during this time.  See newspaper account.

See also this page for descriptions of other local disturbances including one of an attack on bible teachers near Meigh. These teachers, who travelled the countryside giving bible classes, were funded by wealthy English Evangelicals to convert Catholics to Protestantism and were resented by the local population of Catholics.


In 1948 a number of newspapers report on a rumour of pike making in Meigh with the comment in one of them that the smithy was profiting well from the work.  The said smithy would have been Laurence or Patrick.


Such was the concern of the authorities over local unrest that a decision was taken in 1850 to open two new new police stations, in Meigh and Silverbridge, near Crossmaglen.

The Tithe Applotment books were compiled for all of Ireland between 1823 and 1837. They record the results of the ordnance survey mentioned on the previous page to determine the amount of tax payable by each occupier of land, regardless of his/her religion, to support the clergy of the Church of Ireland. This survey was carried out by the military and in 1834 the Newry Telegraph tells us of an incident regarding the soldiers who were conducting the survey around Meigh. First we have a paragraph telling us that Anne Connellan was fined for keeping her pub open until 2 o’clock in the morning.

“Jonathan Seaver . . . Proprietor let by the statute acre a from 25 shillings to 36 shillings (except the bog). The townland is let about 250 per cent above my valuaton; well circumstanced for roads and …=  from Newry 4 miles.”
