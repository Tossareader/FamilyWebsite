Anne Connellan was widowed some time between 1829 and 1834. We know that she was born in 1775 or earlier and died in 1875 as she is mentioned in the Newry Reporter in 2011 as having reached the age of 100. She would have been about 59 in 1834 at the time of the Applotment books and any children she had would have reached adulthood. We cannot be sure of the number of children she might have had.


The Upper Killeavy Baptism and Marriage Registers for the years 1832-68 are extant but wouldn’t have reference to any children born to Anne. The  entries in it would be for marriages of her children and baptisms of their offspring. The ledger is in very poor condition and difficult to read. Christian and surnames are abbreviated. However in the Baptism Register, each entry is referenced by the village or townland the parents are from and the mother is always identified by her maiden name. Thus any children of Anne and Michael Connellan who married and had children in Meigh can be identified. While there are Connollys mentioned there is a consistency to entrants identified as Conly which seems to be the priest’s shorthand for Connellan.  Thus we find two couples who have children baptised in Meigh between the years 1836 and 1872. These are Lawrence & Mary Hanlon and Patrick & Mary McGinnis.  We have Laurence Connellan who marries in 1835 and has seven children between 1836 and 1851 and Patrick Connellan who marries in 1848 and has ten children between 1851 and 1872. Since these two brothers can be associated with Anne Connellan and the public house through other identifying factors, it is safe to say that these two are the only offspring of Anne and Michael we can be sure of. The entries for marriages in the register point to a fairly large number of Conly’s as marriage partners, witnesses to marriages and also sponsors to baptisms. These could be from any part of the large parish of Upper Killeavy. Whether or not they are related to the Connellan family in Meigh village cannot be ascertained.


As sponsors with the suranme Conly to these baptisms, we have Margaret, Mary, Thomas, Catherine, Michael, Hugh, Owen, and Anne. There are also marriages of 2 Owens, a Mary and a Judith. Are these brothers and sisters of Laurence and Patrick living at home with their mother Anne, or are they cousins from round about? We can’t know.   


So of Michael and Anne Connellan’s possible offspring we can only be sure of Laurence and Patrick and, of their children, we can only be sure of Patrick’s.  Come the 1901 census, only Patrick’s children are living in Meigh. One can only assume the missing family members or relatives were part of the high mortality rate of the famine years (1845-1851). Keeping a public house and a forge, the Connellan family may well have been spared extreme hunger, but they must have been subject to the hardships and disease resultant from it. Certainly of Mary’s 10 children there are only 5 still alive to be listed in the 1901 census. Of Laurence’s family there appears to be no trace. In the 1901 census there are 3 Connellan families listed in Forkhill area, but the ages and names don’t correspond to Laurence’s children.  In the 1911 census the only Connellan’s listed for Co Armagh are those in Meigh.


Laurence must have been the elder of the two since he married 13 years before Patrick. We have death dates for Laurence and Patrick to be found in the Calendar of Wills and Adminstrations in the National Archives and we also have Patrick’s will. Laurence dies in 1861 and Patrick in 1882.  Patrick is our great-great grandfather.  Further information on Laurence and Patrick can be found on later pages


1852 brings us the next information we have, the time of the sale of the encumbered Seaver estate. Many of the landed estates were heavily mortgaged after the famine and Parliamentary Acts were passed allowing the sale of such properties. Between 1849 and 1857 some 3,000 estates changed hands.  (The Seaver Estate was acquired by Peter McDonnell and after his death by Dr James Kelly)







1864 is the next information we can access. This is Griffiths Valuation. Here we have two Connellan households listed for Meigh, Patrick Connellan and Mary Connellan. Patrick’s holdings correspond to those listed for his mother in the Seaver sale and Mary’s correspond with those listed for Laurence. From we can assume that the Mary Connellan listed in Griffiths is Laurence’s widow. Patrick is living in the public house and Mary is listed as having the forge. From this we can surmise that Laurence, the elder who married first moved out and had his own household across the road and that Patrick on marrying continued to live with his mother in the main house. We do not know when Anne relinquished control over the pub and the landed rented. The license for the public house was in Anne’s name until her death in 1875 some 11 years later. Patrick applied for a transfer of the license in 1876, six years before his own death. At the time of Patrick’s marriage,  Ann would have been 80. At the time of Griffiths Valuation in 1864, she would have been 96. She is not listed as the land holder in Griffiths.  Anne died in 1875, seven years before her son Patrick.  






In the advertisement papers for this sale the renters are listed and the land they rent is indicated on accompanying maps. So in the section for Meigh we have the Widow Connolly and Laurence Connolly. Laurence is listed as starting to rent in 1843 and Anne is listed as a tenant from year to year. Whether the land Laurence was now renting was from land previously rented by Anne or whether it was new rentals acquired by him, we have no way of knowing. There could well be others of Anne’s sons and daughters who lived with her and worked in the pub, in the forge and on the farm but these would not be listed as only the main person renting would be on the books.  One of these would be Patrick. In 1852 Anne would have been 84 and was obviously still in control. (The advertisement details can be viewed from Laurence’s page)


1854 brings us the first mention we have of Patrick outside of the parish register. The Belfast Mercury newspaper under the heading “Outrage” tells us that “Patrick Connolly and his brother (both blacksmiths) were “fearfully beaten” at the Turnpike near Ravensdale along with the keeper of the turnpike Charles Madders.” Turnpikes were a system of road tolls in force that the time.  We assume this was Patrick and most likely Laurence.

The list of residents and farmers seems to confine itself to the village of Meigh only as there are only 7 on this list. Mary is listed here again as one of the farmers.


1901 is the next date of public record - the Census. There are 43 families listed for Meigh village  but the only Connellan or Connolly is Patrick’s family. One can only assume the missing family members or relatives were part of the high mortality rate of the famine years (1845-1851). Of Patrick and Mary’s 10 children there are only 5 still alive to be listed in the 1901 census. Of Laurence’s family there appears to be no trace. As has been noted elsewhere the 1901 census has 3 families with the surname Connellan in the Forkhill areas. Some names correspond to Laurence’s children but the ages don’t match. In the 1911 census the only Connellan’s listed for Co Armagh are those in Meigh.


Anne dies in 1875. We know this because of an article in the an 1911 edition of the Newry Reporter (see below). This article cites a number of people who “attained the age of 107”.  The number of 107 is so arbitrary as to be  probably a misprint  for 100. The people listed can’t all have died aged 107.  Allowing Anne to be 100 in 1875 would fit with her being a daughter of Peter and Alicia O’Neill from Clonlum. Since Alicia O’Neill died in 1835 aged, we are told, 82, Anne would have had to be the eldest which would mean Alicia had married and had her first child at 19.  One might also add that given the lack of written records, there can never be exact certainty about dates.

Slater’s Directory, published in 1894 some 6 years after Bassetts,  similarly lists Mary as spirit retailer and Thomas as blacksmith.

Twenty years later in 1881 we have Patrick’s will where the forge is included with the land and houses he bequeaths to his family. Some six years after Patrick’s death, in Bassett’s Directory for 1888 we have Meigh listed as a village of eight houses with Mary Connellan, Patrick’s widow, as spirit retailer and Thomas Connellan, her son, as blacksmith. Of the 29 Farmers and Residents listed for the Killeavy area in Bassett’s Directory there are only 4 for Meigh. There is no other Connolly or Connellan. Has Laurence’s wife Mary died by then? And what of Laurence’s children? There are also no longer any O’Neill’s in Clonlum.
